The Night Witches

Who Were They?

The Night Witches were the all-female 588th Night Bomber Regiment of the Soviet Air Forces during World War II. Composed entirely of women volunteers, they flew over 23,000 missions and dropped more than 3,000 tons of bombs on invading German forces.

Flying in wood-and-canvas biplanes, often without parachutes, they completed their missions under the cover of darkness and struck terror into the hearts of their enemies with stealthy and relentless attacks.

Timeline of Their Bravery

1941

Formation of the 588th Regiment by Marina Raskova.

1942

First combat mission. Nickname "Night Witches" given by Germans.

1943

Record number of missions flown — over 1,000 in one month.

1945

End of WWII. Regiment disbanded with honor. Numerous members awarded medals.

Legacy and Recognition

The Night Witches demonstrated extraordinary courage and resilience. Despite facing discrimination and using outdated equipment, their impact was undeniable. Thirty of their members died in combat, and many were awarded the Hero of the Soviet Union medal.

They remain a symbol of bravery, tenacity, and the power of women in wartime history.

Notable Members

Among the most famous were Marina Raskova, a pioneering aviator who helped form the regiment, and Nadezhda Popova, a squadron commander who flew 852 missions. Their stories continue to inspire generations.

These women paved the way for greater inclusion of women in military roles around the world.

“We were called ‘Night Witches’ because we flew only in the dark. But we weren’t witches — we were girls who wanted to do our part for our country.”
